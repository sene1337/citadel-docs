⚡ The Problem We're Solving

A Bitcoin-focused founder running a family office ("the Citadel") spans multiple companies, a Bitcoin angel investing portfolio, properties, a non-profit, a team of human operators, and an AI agent. As of today, there is no operating system — work is scattered across 6+ disconnected tools with zero shared visibility.

This document builds the machine.

Companies & Ventures

  • A Bitcoin nonprofit
  • Multiple portfolio companies in various stages
  • Family / household projects and initiatives

Investments

The Deeper Problem

The original instinct is to treat this as a company-level project management problem. It's not.

A founder running a family office doesn't run one company — they run 8+ life domains simultaneously. The real problem is the absence of a personal operating system that sits above all the companies and entities.

What actually happens without one: the founder gets overwhelmed and falls behind. Things don't get done. Standards slip. Accountability drops. Then reactive mode kicks in — putting out fires instead of building systems, managing crises instead of executing strategy. The cycle repeats because there's no structure to break it.

⚠️ The most expensive vacancy in your org is the one that's making you do everyone else's job. Every day without a Chief of Staff is a day the Founder is managing 8 domains, multiple direct reports, cross-domain Rocks, and system design — instead of doing strategy and capital allocation.

Cross-domain priorities that span multiple entities or life areas

But the Ops Partner lacks DRI authority across personal taxes + multiple companies + non-profit. The Founder owns it. The Ops Partner can own a component scoped to investments.

🎯 The CoS Seat — Gap Analysis

🔴 The CoS seat is the most expensive vacancy in the Citadel. The Founder is filling it by default — managing 8 domains, multiple direct reports, cross-domain Rocks, and system design instead of doing strategy and capital allocation.

What the CoS Owns

  • Citadel OS operations — owns the system, keeps it running
  • Manages EA + Operations Partner day-to-day
  • Owns cross-domain Rocks
  • Financial Catchup coordination
  • Weekly rhythm enforcement across all domains

Hire Profile

  • Loves chaos — doesn't shrink from ambiguity
  • Context-switches as fast as the Founder
  • Proactively builds systems before fires start
  • High execution, high trust, full Citadel visibility
  • NOT a reactive inbox-manager
  • NOT someone who needs stable, single-domain scope

Build First, Then Hire

The AI Agent builds the Citadel OS infrastructure first. CoS walks into a working system — not an undefined "figure it out" mandate where scope collapses under pressure.

This is the vacuum hire warning: when a role is hired into a vacuum, it gravitates toward whatever inbound pressure is highest (usually the loudest domain), leaving the systems layer permanently unbuilt.

The Strategic Question

Can an existing team member grow into CoS, or is this a new hire?

An Ops Partner who has been operating in depth-focused mode for 12+ months is likely gravitational — that operating mode will resist a breadth/chaos management role. Their instincts may surface CoS-level insights, but the seat needs someone who thrives on breadth.

💡 Recommendation: Keep Ops Partner in their lane. Hire CoS separately. Fractional CoS is viable as an interim while building trust with a full-time hire.

⚙️ Layer 2 — Execution Playbook (Dan Martell Framework)

The execution engine that runs inside each domain. Synthesized from extensive Elite, Quantum, and Mighty coaching transcripts. This is the full operational playbook — not a summary.

Vision (25-yr) → Annual Plan → Quarterly Rocks → Weekly Scorecard → Daily Rhythm

The System: Idea → Rock → Scorecard → Execution → Completion

1
25-Year Vision → 3-Year → 1-Year Plan
Start with a 25-year vision, work backward to a 3-year plan, then a 1-year plan. Get the sequencing right before breaking into quarters.
2
Break the annual plan into quarterly Rocks
Each quarter gets 3–5 Rocks (ideally 3). These are the big, important items — not day-to-day work — that move things forward.
3
Each Rock gets a SMART goal + strategies + tactics + DRI
Specific, measurable, attainable, relevant, time-bound. Below the SMART goal are 3–5 strategies and supporting tactics. One person (the DRI) owns each Rock.
4
Rocks feed into the weekly scorecard review
Each week, the team reviews Rocks as red/green/yellow. The DRI reports status. If red, they explain what's blocked and what they're doing about it.
5
Scorecard tracks leading/lagging metrics weekly
3–5 metrics per department, updated weekly by the DRI who owns that number. Red (>10% below goal), Yellow (within 10%), Green (at or above).
6
Daily/weekly meetings enforce rhythm
Daily admin sync for CEO + assistant. Weekly team sync for scorecard review. Department meetings as needed.
7
Systems lock in completed work
Once a process is proven, document it (Camcorder Method), turn it into an SOP, and delegate. This "system stacking" frees the CEO to focus on the next Rock.

Rocks System

What are Rocks? Big, important projects you plan for first in your calendar — not everyday work. Fill the glass with big rocks before sand/pebbles, or they won't fit.

Rock Temple Framework (Tod Melnyk)

Draw a house: peak at top, 3–5 pillars in the middle, base at bottom.

  • Top: Q metrics/goals (revenue, clients, profit — what you're delivering by end of quarter)
  • Pillars: The Rocks — each with SMART goal, strategies, tactics, and DRI
  • Base: Always "Talent & Culture" — you can't execute without great people

Structure of each Rock

  • SMART goal (e.g., "By March 31, deliver $2M in revenue")
  • 3–5 strategies
  • Supporting tactics
  • Monthly milestones (Month 1 target, Month 2 target, Month 3 target)
  • One DRI who is accountable
"At the end of the quarter, what would make me sit back and go 'holy fuck, that just clearly changed things from where we were 90 days ago?' If you can't say that about your Rocks, you have too many or the wrong ones."

Scorecard System

What it is: A spreadsheet (or tool like Precision.co) tracking 3–5 metrics per department, updated weekly, reviewed in the weekly team meeting.

Rules

1
Every number has a DRI
One person owns updating and reporting each metric. They update it themselves to stay connected to the number.
2
Colors are mandatory
Glance at the sheet, instantly see what's red. No hiding behind averages.
3
Goals are negotiated
"If I build the plan, they'll fight the plan. If they build the plan, they won't fight the plan." CEO negotiates targets with the team — they propose numbers, CEO pressure-tests.
4
It's public
Every team member can see every number. "Sunlight sanitizes."
"Scorecards don't lie. People respect what you inspect. Great teams want to know the score."

System Stacking

Concept: Take tasks you're doing repeatedly, document them, and "lock them in" via a person or automation — then stack the next one on top.

BUSY
40+ things/week, no systems
→
SYSTEM STACK
One task off your plate per week, forever
→
PRODUCTIVE
Machine runs; you do high-leverage work

S.A.D. Process — Systemize, Automate, Delegate

S
Systemize
Create the system/process — checklist, steps, SOP
A
Automate
Bring in software/AI to handle repetitive parts
D
Delegate
Assign ownership (DRI) to a person. Not just the task — the entire system, automation, scorecard, and accountability.
"Delegate isn't a person per se. Delegate is I now have somebody that owns the process. It's about ownership."

Systems Builder — DRY Framework

DRY (Don't Repeat Yourself): If you solve a problem, document it so you never solve it again.

Three Building Blocks

1
Code (Checklists)
The repeatable steps. Like a recipe — follow it, get the result 100% of the time.
2
Config Files (Preferences)
A document of all personal preferences — shirt size, plane seat, hotel, communication style, calendar rules. Referenced by many checklists. Change it in one place, updates everywhere. A "Working with [Name]" doc every new hire reads.
3
Modules (Reusable Processes)
A process that multiple departments reference. E.g., "client gifting module" used by Customer Success, Sales, and EA. Write once, reference from many playbooks.

Four Components of a Complete System

  • Steps — the checklist/process
  • Scorecard — quality, quantity, and timeline measurements
  • Training — Camcorder Method recordings + regular drills
  • Owner — a DRI who owns the whole thing

Belief Blockers

"Nobody can do what I do" → Document your process and anyone can learn it.
"They'll mess up" → 80% done by someone else is 100% awesome. Dan's team lost $142K from a mistake — he asked "is this a learn once?" and moved on.
"I can't build systems, I'm a creative" → YouTube + help files + 3 hours of focus = you can learn anything.
"It'll get stale" → Keep it simple. The DRI who owns it updates it.

Camcorder Method

Phase 1: Capture

  • Outline the video — write high-level beats
  • Record yourself doing the work — Zoom, share screen, talk through it. You're doing the work anyway, just recording it.
  • Save in a folder — even if you're not ready to delegate yet

Phase 2: Codify

  • Transcribe (Rev, Descript, or similar)
  • AI creates the SOP — paste transcript: "Create an SOP using this transcript." Gets it 92% there.
  • Delegate — give the SOP + original video to the person. Video has micro-moments the SOP can't capture.

Turbo Version

  • Criteria — list 5–7 things that must be true for output to be great (Definition of Done)
  • Feedback Form — scoring rubric (1–5 per criterion)
  • Collect resources — reference materials, examples, templates
  • Record — standard Camcorder Method
  • Hand off — recording + criteria + feedback form + resources
  • They create the SOP — not you. They watch, draft, Q&A with you, then own it.
"I haven't created a system in years. As soon as I create a system for somebody else's department, I'm doing their job."

Meeting Cadences

Daily Admin Sync (Founder + EA)

1
My List
Running document with sections per direct report. Add thoughts as they arise throughout the day.
2
Calendar Review
Mondays: 6 weeks forward. Rest of week: 2 weeks forward.
3
Review Folder
Emails needing Founder decision. Founder reviews and decides.
4
Past Meetings
EA goes through every meeting since last sync. "You talked to X — anything to do there?" Founder never thinks about follow-up.
5
Project Updates
EA updates on things they're managing.

Key rule: EA runs the meeting, not the Founder. "I don't like to run meetings. I like to participate in meetings."

Weekly Leadership Sync (Whole Team)

  • Wins — share team/personal wins
  • Scorecard Review — each DRI reports their numbers (red/green/yellow)
  • Rock Review — each Rock DRI reports status, identifies blockers
  • Customer Wins / Headlines — company updates, notable stories
  • Issues — anyone can add items; team works through them
  • Updates — personal progress, project updates, things learned

CEO vs Team Roles

The CEO's Four Areas

  • People — hiring, developing, coaching
  • Reporting — scorecard, metrics, knowing the numbers
  • Strategy — vision, Rocks, sequencing
  • Systems — overseeing the playbooks, processes, culture

The 80/10 Rule: CEO does the first 10% (creative direction) and the final 10% (review/approval). Team does the 80% in between.

What the CEO does NOT do

  • Create systems (team creates, CEO reviews)
  • Run meetings (assistant or team leads run them)
  • Answer day-to-day operational questions
  • Manage people minute-to-minute
  • Own more than 5 direct reports (7 max)

⚠️ Pitfalls & Design Principles

These are anti-patterns the system must be designed to make impossible.

🎛️
Don't micromanage how people track their own tasks
The system tracks outcomes and scorecard metrics — not how individuals organize their work.
🔬
Don't build visibility into minutia that won't provide value
Scorecard items must be meaningful. If the Founder doesn't care about a number, it's not on the scorecard.
😐
Don't be paralyzed by scale and scope
Start with one domain running the rhythm. Stack the next one on top. System Stacking, not System Designing.
🤖
Don't be paralyzed by the complexity of partnering with AI
The AI Agent owns the system. The Founder calls the plays. Keep the human/AI boundary clear and trust the files.
⏱️
Don't spend 3 days on a playbook playbook
The Founder cannot be the bottleneck. If building a system takes longer than using it, stop and ship something shippable.
📈
The system must scale from simple to complex
Works for workspace cleanup on day 1 AND a full family office portfolio on day 100. Design for both ends.
🔄
Don't hire into a vacuum (the Vacuum Hire pattern)
Build Citadel OS infrastructure first. CoS walks into a working system with defined scope — not an undefined "figure it out" mandate.
📝 Decision Log — System of Record

Problem: No operating system. 6+ tools, multiple people, zero visibility, no handoff standard.

Options Evaluated

A
All-in on one PM tool — HIGH RISK. Tool wasn't the problem; organizational behavior was. Fails without system culture change.
B
Hub-and-spoke scorecard — LOW RISK but insufficient. Solves visibility, not durability or handoffs.
C
Build custom AI-native system — HIGH RISK. Premature. Building PM tool instead of running projects.
D ✓
Unified system of record + scorecard rhythm — CHOSEN. Both a single system of record (durability, handoffs) AND a scorecard rhythm (visibility, accountability). Tool choice is a separate evaluation.

💡 The key pushback that elevated the decision: "If someone leaves, their silo goes with them. What if we need to replace anyone?" This requirement — handoff-readiness — elevated from "just visibility" to "unified system of record."
