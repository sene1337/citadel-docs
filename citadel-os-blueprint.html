The Deeper Problem (Discovered March 4, 2026)

The original v1 plan treated this as a company-level project management problem. It's not. Brad doesn't run one company — he runs 8 life domains simultaneously. The real problem is the absence of a personal operating system that sits above all the companies and entities.

The team has been operating without a shared operating system. Everyone is capable and doing real work — but without a unified system, coordination happens ad hoc and things fall through the cracks. The investment and portfolio work naturally created the most inbound pressure, so that's where bandwidth concentrated. The broader systems layer — playbooks, accountability rhythm, cross-domain coordination — never had a chance to form.

The system infrastructure IS forming now (playbooks, decision architecture, SOPs), but it needs to be built out before anyone can reliably operate across all domains.

🔩 Layer 2 — Execution Playbook Dan Martell Framework

The full execution engine that runs inside each domain. Synthesized from 15+ Elite, Quantum, and Mighty coaching transcripts. This is the operational playbook — not a summary.

The Blueprint
Vision (25-yr) → Annual Plan → Quarterly Rocks → Weekly Scorecard → Daily Rhythm

The System: Idea → Rock → Scorecard → Execution → Completion

  1. 25-Year Vision → 3-Year → 1-Year Plan. Start with a 25-year vision, work backward to a 3-year plan, then a 1-year plan. Get the sequencing right.
  2. Break the annual plan into quarterly Rocks. Each quarter gets 3–5 Rocks (ideally 3). These are the big, important items — not day-to-day work — that move things forward.
  3. Each Rock gets a SMART goal + strategies + tactics + DRI. Specific, measurable, attainable, relevant, time-bound. Below the SMART goal are 3–5 strategies and supporting tactics. One person (the DRI) owns each Rock.
  4. Rocks feed into the weekly scorecard review. Each week, the team reviews Rocks as red/green/yellow. The DRI reports status. If red, they explain what's blocked and what they're doing about it.
  5. The scorecard tracks leading/lagging metrics weekly. 3–5 metrics per department, updated weekly by the DRI who owns that number. Red (>10% below goal), Yellow (within 10%), Green (at or above).
  6. Daily/weekly meetings enforce rhythm. Daily admin sync for CEO + assistant. Weekly team sync for scorecard review. Department meetings as needed.
  7. Systems are built to lock in completed work. Once a process is proven, it gets documented (Camcorder Method), turned into an SOP, and delegated. This "system stacking" frees the CEO to focus on the next Rock.

Rocks System

🪨
What are Rocks?
Big, important projects you plan for first in your calendar — not everyday work. Fill the glass with big rocks before sand/pebbles, or they won't fit. 3–5 per quarter; 3 is ideal for focus.
🏛️
Rock Temple (Tod Melnyk)
Draw a house: Peak = Q metrics/goals. Pillars = the 3–5 Rocks, each with SMART goal + strategies + DRI. Base = always "Talent & Culture" — can't execute without great people.
🎯
Structure of Each Rock
SMART goal · 3–5 strategies · Supporting tactics · Monthly milestones (Jan/Feb/Mar) · One DRI who is accountable. One Rock should be "net new" — a genuinely new initiative.
📡
Cascading Rocks
Company Rocks cascade down. If someone owns a creative Rock, their team's Rocks should support it — not be independent side projects. If a Rock spans authority boundaries, it belongs one level up.

How Rocks Are Tracked

  • Added to the weekly leadership sync agenda
  • Each DRI reports red / green / yellow on their Rock every week
  • If red: identify the blocker, present the plan to fix it
  • Team has the right to ask questions
  • Each Rock gets a one-word descriptor (e.g., "Quality," "Book," "Customers") — instant recognition
"At the end of the quarter, what would make me sit back and go 'holy fuck, that just clearly changed things from where we were 90 days ago?' If you can't say that about your Rocks, you have too many or the wrong ones."

Scorecard System

A spreadsheet (or tool like Precision.co) tracking 3–5 metrics per department, updated weekly, reviewed in the weekly team meeting.

Structure

  • Rows: metrics (traffic, qualified leads, booked calls, close rate, revenue, churn)
  • Columns: Week 1–4 → Month → Quarter → Year
  • Colors: Red (>10% below target) · Yellow (within 10%) · Green (at or above)
  • Everything's a funnel — Marketing → Sales → Customer Success. Also a Talent Funnel. Both on the scorecard.
👤
Every Number Has a DRI
One person owns updating and reporting each metric. They update it themselves to stay connected to the number. Not a shared spreadsheet — an owned number.
🤝
Goals Are Negotiated
"If I build the plan, they'll fight the plan. If they build the plan, they won't fight the plan." CEO pressure-tests; team proposes numbers. Shared ownership of the target.
☀️
It's Public
Every team member can see every number. "Sunlight sanitizes." No selective reporting. Great teams want to know the score.
🛠️
Tool
Start with a Google Sheet. For companies doing $300K+/month with a sales team: Precision.co. Colors are mandatory — glance at the sheet, instantly see what's red.
"Scorecards don't lie. People respect what you inspect. Great teams want to know the score."

System Stacking

Take tasks you're doing repeatedly, document them, and "lock them in" via a person or automation — then stack the next one on top.

The Progression

1. Busy
→
2. System Stack
→
3. Productive

Doing 40+ things/week with no systems → each week, take one task off your plate permanently → machine runs; you focus on high-leverage work.

How to Stack

  1. Pick one task you do repeatedly
  2. Make a commitment: "I'm never doing this again"
  3. Document it (Camcorder Method — record yourself doing it)
  4. Give it to a person or automate it
  5. Move to the next task
⚠️
Time Assassins (what prevents stacking): Vices · Not capturing tasks for delegation · Perfectionism ("it won't be as good as when I do it") · Shiny object syndrome (starting new things instead of locking in current ones)

S.A.D. Process — Systemize, Automate, Delegate

📋
1. Systemize
Create the system / process — checklist, steps, SOP. Define what "done" looks like and how to get there repeatably.
🤖
2. Automate
Bring in software or AI to handle repetitive parts. Reduce human touchpoints for mechanical steps. Tools serve the system.
👋
3. Delegate
Assign ownership — a DRI who owns the entire process: the system, the automation, the scorecard, and the accountability. Not just the task.
"Delegate isn't a person per se. Delegate is I now have somebody that owns the process. It's about ownership."

Systems Builder — DRY Framework

💡
DRY: Don't Repeat Yourself. If you solve a problem, document it so you never solve it again.

Three Building Blocks

📝
1. Code (Checklists)
The repeatable steps. Like a recipe — follow it, get the result 100% of the time. The foundation of any system.
⚙️
2. Config Files (Preferences)
A document of all personal preferences — shirt size, plane seat, hotel, communication style, calendar rules. Change it in one place, updates everywhere. Referenced by many checklists.
🔁
3. Modules (Reusable Processes)
A process that multiple departments reference. E.g., "client gifting module" used by Customer Success, Sales, and EA. Write once, reference from many playbooks.

Four Components of a Complete System

  1. Steps — the checklist/process
  2. Scorecard — quality, quantity, and timeline measurements
  3. Training — Camcorder Method recordings + regular drills
  4. Owner — a DRI who owns the whole thing

Progression

Program Doc (big dump of everything) → System (specific SOP for frequent processes) → Playbook (complete operating system for the business)

Belief Blockers Dan Busts

  • "Nobody can do what I do" → Document your process and anyone can learn it.
  • "They'll mess up" → 80% done by someone else is 100% awesome. Dan's team lost $142K from a mistake — he asked "is this a learn once?" and moved on.
  • "I can't build systems, I'm a creative" → YouTube + help files + 3 hours of focus = you can learn anything.
  • "It'll get stale" → Keep it simple. The DRI who owns it updates it.

Camcorder Method

🎥
Phase 1: Capture
  1. Outline the video — write high-level beats
  2. Record yourself doing the work — Zoom, share screen, talk through it. Net Time: you're doing the work anyway.
  3. Save in a folder — even if you're not ready to delegate yet
📄
Phase 2: Codify
  1. Transcribe (Rev, Descript, or similar)
  2. AI creates the SOP — paste transcript: "Create an SOP using this transcript." Gets it 92% there.
  3. Delegate — give SOP + original video to the person. Video captures micro-moments the SOP can't.

⚡ Turbo Version

  1. Criteria — list 5–7 things that must be true for output to be great (Definition of Done)
  2. Feedback Form — scoring rubric (1–5 per criterion)
  3. Collect resources — reference materials, examples, templates
  4. Record — standard Camcorder Method
  5. Hand off — recording + criteria + feedback form + resources
  6. They create the SOP — not you. They watch, draft, Q&A with you, then own it.
"I haven't created a system in years. As soon as I create a system for somebody else's department, I'm doing their job."

CEO vs Team Roles

👑
The CEO's Four Areas
  • People — hiring, developing, coaching
  • Reporting — scorecard, metrics, knowing the numbers
  • Strategy — vision, Rocks, sequencing
  • Systems — overseeing playbooks, processes, culture
⚡
The 80/10 Rule
CEO does the first 10% (creative direction) and the final 10% (review/approval). Team does the 80% in between. CEO does NOT create systems — team creates, CEO reviews. CEO does NOT run meetings — assistant or team leads run them.
🎯
What the Team Does
Owns Rocks as DRIs. Updates scorecard weekly. Creates and maintains SOPs. Runs meetings and agendas. Makes decisions within their domain. Cross-trains and runs skills drills.
🚫
What the CEO Does NOT Do
Create systems · Run meetings · Answer day-to-day operational questions · Manage people minute-to-minute · Own more than 5 direct reports (7 max)

⛔ Pitfalls — Brad's Words

These came directly from Brad. They're anti-patterns, not aspirations. The system must be designed to make all of these impossible to fall into.

🎛️ Don't micromanage how people keep track of their own tasks. The system tracks outcomes and scorecard metrics — not how individuals organize their work.
🔬 Don't build visibility into minutia that won't provide value. Scorecard items must be meaningful. If Brad doesn't care about a number, it's not on the scorecard.
😐 Don't be paralyzed by scale and scope. Start with one domain running the rhythm. Stack the next one on top. System Stacking, not System Designing.
🤖 Don't be paralyzed by the complexity of partnering with AI. Sene owns the system. Brad calls the plays. Keep the human/AI boundary clear and trust the files.
⏱️ Don't spend 3 days on a playbook playbook. Brad cannot be the bottleneck. If building a system takes longer than using it, stop and ship something shippable.
📈 The system must scale from simple to complex. Works for workspace cleanup on day 1 AND family office with 50+ investments on day 100. Design for both ends.
🔄 Don't hire into a vacuum (the Anton pattern again). Build Citadel OS infrastructure first. CoS walks into a working system with defined scope, not an undefined "figure it out" mandate.
